Our Mission

Advocates for Universal Merit, AUM, is a nonprofit founded to support the Monk-Life community, an online group of Buddhist practitioners affiliated with the ThaBarWa international Buddhist Sangha, providing the four requisites of food, clothing, shelter and medicine and supporting special projects for doing good deeds across the United States and abroad.

Initiatives

With the Buddhist principles of mindfulness and detachment as a foundation, ThaBarWa emphasizes the doing of good deeds for all without any limits or exclusions. No one is refused help because of age, gender, ethnicity, mental or physical disability, or an inability to pay.

Our Story

Advocates for Universal Merit, AUM, is a nonprofit that was founded in order to support Monk-Life's and ThaBarWa's community members.
Here's the story of how it all started:

  • October, 2002

    Sayadaw Ashin Ottamathara Ordains

    By 2002, the founder of the ThaBarWa organization, born in Myanmar in 1969, had already successfully completed a degree in English and had been running his own chain of convenience stores, responsible for hundreds of employees. Disenchanted with his lack of involvement in helping others, he had taken up an intense, committed meditation practice three years earlier. In October of 2002, he left behind his previous life and ordained as a Buddhist monk. Due to the diligence of his efforts as a meditator, he was named a Vipassana meditation teacher by the Abbot of Devasagara Monastery in Yangon.

  • 2007

    The First Thabarwa Center Established

    Only five years after his Ordination, Sayadaw opened his first Thabarwa Meditation Center. ThaBarWa is a Myanmar word that means "nature" and points to Sayadaw's understanding of the potential for unlimited good inherent in all beings. With his own meditation center, students who had been following him from class to class held at various private homes, hospitals and monasteries now had a place to stay, practice meditation and do good deeds freely. At Thabarwa, he has taught a growing number of male (monks) and female (nuns) monastics, yogis (nonmonastic practitioners), patients and anyone in need how to cultivate the ability of doing good deeds without limits as a means of solving problems for themselves and society.

  • 2018

    Bhante Varrapanyo Ordains

    In 2014, a young American man of 20 years began his spiritual journey by spending a year at a Zen Buddhist meditation center in New Mexico. His early years of practice were marked by intensive meditation training and a deep immersion into Buddhist teachings. In 2018, he moved to Thailand to ordain as a Bhikkhu in the Thai Forrest Dhammayut tradition at Wat Phra Dara Philom Forrest Monastery and was given the name Bhante Varrapanyo. As he further deepened his commitment to Buddhism, he traveled to many temples in Asia to study and practice. Bhante subsequently met Sayadaw Ashin Ottamathara and adopted his approach to the practice of Buddhism and the service of others. He now serves ThaBarWa as a Dharma Teacher, focusing on alleviating others’ suffering through meditation and community.

  • 2020

    The Monk-Life Online Community is Born

    Aspiring to serve others in the midst of the distress of the outbreak of the Covid pandemic, Bhante established the online Monk-Life Community using Discord and other platforms. He maintains this online community as a place for those interested in Buddhism, meditation,lovingkindness and service. The server is run by a himself, a fully ordained Buddhist Monk and an engaged group of lay practitioners. Live meditation sessions and Dharma classes are available on a daily basis. Anyone interested in the practice of meditation and bringing peace into their lives are welcome.

Housing

A Community Built on Compassionate Inclusion

The Thabarwa organization is unique in its approach to helping people who find themselves without housing, either through political upheaval or life's circumstances. In hundreds of Thabarwa centers in multiple countries across several continents, people of all ages, backgrounds and abilities find shelter. No one is turned away because of gender, age, ethnicity, mental or physical disability or an inability to pay. Community members support each other through acts of kindness, each contributing what they have to offer, and the guideliness for behavior are founded on Buddhist ethics.

Orphanages

A Safe Haven

Thabarwa Centers have become known safe havens for children who have either been orphaned or abandoned. The children, sometime as early as infancy, are provided with food, shelter, clothing, housing and education. They grow up with the community of monastics and lay practitioners serving as their extended family.

Monasteries

Buddhist Monastic Communities

In addition to the aid being given to the lay members of the society in which they are found, the many Thabarwa locations around the world serve as Meditation Centers where men and women who wish to commit themselves fully to Buddhist practice can ordain and adopt a life of communal living. Following the Buddhist tradition that goes back thousands of years, monastics typically go out into the surrounding community in the morning to collect alms. Through the generosity of the people in the area, the food and donations received feed not only support the monastics who begged for alms but also are shared with the larger group of those who are finding shelter at the center. With their basic requisites provided for, monastics then have to opportunity to devote themselves to the practice of meditation, chanting, study and teaching.
